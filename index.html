Sleep Schedule

An app to aid and track sleep.

Why Do We Sleep?

Sleep is a period of rest for both your brain and your body, serving two main functions: protein synthesis throughout the body and maintaining neural plasticity in the brain. This allows for your body to recover and your mind to consolidate new memories from the day before with old material.

What Happens When We Sleep?

Sleep cycles through a succession of different states of consciousness, which are recorded through electroencephalograms (EEGs).

When you are awake, your brain functions on alpha and beta waves depending on alertness.

Stage 1

As you fall asleep, you become relaxed, enter the hypnagogic state, and do not respond to external stimuli. This begins Non-REM-1 (NREM 1), which consist of theta brain waves. During short naps, your brain usually enters this state of consciousness and is woken up around this state because it is the lightest form of sleep.

Stage 2

Passing into NREM-2, your brain exhibits high-frequency bursts of activity, known as sleep spindles.

Stage 3

When you enter deeper sleep in NREM-3, the EEGs show mostly delta waves as your heart rate, respiration, temperature, and blood flow are reduced. This is also when your body begins repairing itself with growth hormone involved in maintaining physiological functions.

After reaching stage 3, you cycle back up through to stage 1 and into Rapid Eye Movement (REM) sleep after 90 minutes. This is usually when you begin dreaming. Your eyes move rapidly, your breathing becomes shallow and irregularly rapid, your heart rate and blood pressure increases, and your muscles become paralyzed, mirror in your dreaming pattern while preventing sleep walking.
